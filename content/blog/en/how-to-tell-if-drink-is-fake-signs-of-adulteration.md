# How to tell if drink is fake: essential protection

Counterfeit beverages represent an invisible danger in bars, stores, informal establishments. The problem is not just fraud, but serious risk: methanol at dangerous levels causes blindness and death. WHO estimates 21% of globally consumed alcohol comes from illegal sources.

This article presents 7 main signs to identify how to tell if drink is fake before consumption. **Additionally**, tools like AlcoLab add an extra layer of protection against adulteration.

## Sign 1: Price far below market

If a beverage costs much less than normal, caution is valid. Counterfeiters charge low to compete. Premium beverages rarely have drastic discounts. Whiskey, cognac, or foreign vodka at a third of price? Question origin immediately.

## Sign 2: Printing errors on label

Poor label quality is classic counterfeiting sign. Observe the label: blurred letters, faded colors, incorrect alignment, different fonts? Brand logo, expiration date, and bar code should be perfectly aligned and sharp.

Counterfeit beverages have labels with cheap technology leaving visible errors. Legitimate brands invest in high-quality printing. **Firstly**, typos are especially suspicious. Established brands would not have spelling errors. Counterfeiters frequently make orthographic mistakes.

## Sign 3: Compromised seal and closure

Open carefully and inspect closure. The seal should be intact, well-glued, perfect. Small signs of violation? Reject completely. Cork or cap well-fitted? Protective plastic intact?

**Red flag:** counterfeiters buy premium empty bottles on eBay. Signs of previous glue removal or imperfectly applied sealing indicate counterfeiting. Examine carefully.

## Sign 4: No regulatory registration

In many countries, legitimate beverages are registered with health authorities. **Therefore**, look for registration number on label. Many include QR codes redirecting to regulatory database.

Without proper registration or unverifiable number? Nearly certain the product is illegal or counterfeit. Check your country's health authority website for details on legitimate beverage registration.

## Sign 5: Unknown or suspicious origin

Distrust informal vendors without invoice or clear origin. If vendor does not know origin, do not buy. Legitimate brands distribute through: licensed stores, authorized distributors, established supermarkets.

Premium beverage from street vendor or small bar? Highly suspect. Always request receipt. Formal stores offer invoice. Informal vendors never do.

## Sign 6: Non-standard packaging

Observe bottle size, weight, shape. Known brands maintain rigid standard. Bottle lighter/heavier or slightly different shape? May indicate counterfeiting.

Counterfeiters use generic bottles similar but not identical. **However**, packaging material matters: good quality glass or fragile? Cheap-looking label plastic? Combined differences indicate counterfeiting.

## Sign 7: Suspicious availability in unusual locations

Premium beverages do not suddenly appear at neighborhood shops. Expensive international brand available at small store that never had it? Valid suspicion.

Counterfeiters stock local vendors with fake inventory for rapid turnover. Vendor cannot explain new stock origin? Do not buy. **After all**, authentic brands follow known distribution pattern, consistently present at authorized locations.

## Global counterfeiting context

Printing technology becomes cheaper annually. Counterfeiters create incredibly realistic labels. Turkey 2025: even known brands' rakı counterfeited with convincing packaging.

**Critical India problem:** premium bottles frequently counterfeited. Counterfeiters buy authentic empty bottles internationally, fill with adulterated beverage (methanol), close false seals well-executed. Sophistication increases.

## The invisible danger: methanol colorless, odorless

**Here is the real risk:** methanol is colorless, odorless, taste similar to ethanol. Impossible to detect visually or by taste if a beverage contains methanol.

A person can drink a counterfeit glass noticing nothing abnormal. Hours later: headache, blurred vision, mental confusion, blindness if severe. **In this sense**, visual identification is only first protection layer. Tools like AlcoLab complement.

## Regulatory verification

Health authorities maintain updated database of registered alcoholic beverages. Consult your country's health authority website personally.

**Modern QR code:** many bottles include codes leading to regulatory registration. Scan before buying. Code does not work or page broken? Sign of counterfeiting.

**Report counterfeits:**
- Health authority (website)
- Local consumer protection agency
- Municipal health authority
- Anonymous tip lines

Reports help enforcement increase effectiveness.
