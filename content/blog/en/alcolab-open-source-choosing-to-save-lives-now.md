## AlcoLab Open Source: Life Now or Profit Later

The creators of [AlcoLab](https://alcolab.org) faced a fundamental crossroads. They didn't choose the traditional innovation path — patents, exclusive licenses, future profits.

Instead, they chose to **save lives now**. They made the technology completely free and **open source**. This decision deserves to be told because it wasn't easy. And its implications go far beyond code.

## The Dilemma: Patent or Immediate Access?

When health innovation emerges, there's an established conventional process:

1. Protect intellectual property via patent
2. Wait 1.5 years for official publication
3. Wait 5-7 years until granting
4. Then monetize the technology

No one would blame the team for following that path. It would be safe, predictable, potentially lucrative.

But there was an uncomfortable problem: **people were being poisoned at that very moment**. In countries where beverage adulteration is brutal reality. How could they wait 7 years while people died?

The answer was a beautiful "no". Therefore, they chose differently. After all, lives don't wait for patents.

## The Decision: Open Code Now

**AlcoLab was published as open source under AGPL-3.0 license**. This choice protects everything:

**AGPL-3.0 means:** any derivation must be open source too. Source code completely available. No loopholes for proprietary exploitation.

But the team was strategic. First, they filed with INPI (National Institute of Industrial Property). This guaranteed priority of date. Only after that did they publish openly.

Furthermore, they deposited research in **Zenodo**, international scientific repository. Why? To prevent third parties from claiming patent on same idea in other jurisdictions.

Therefore, they protected innovation and guaranteed universal access simultaneously. In sum, AlcoLab open source saves lives now.

## The Reality of Costs: Voluntary Support

Here enters the untold part about open-source software.

**Team members personally pay server costs**. No one profits. The server runs because they believe in the mission.

Is this sustainable indefinitely? That's the question haunting any open-source project. In this sense, what happens when initial enthusiasm exhausts itself?

## Silent Sustainability Risks

When open-source projects lack formal support, dangerous scenarios emerge:

### Volunteer Burnout

Dedicated contributors have lives, jobs, families. Maintaining projects for free indefinitely leads to exhaustion. How many developers have abandoned projects?

### Accumulated Technical Debt

Without resources for refactoring, old patterns solidify. Code that works in 2026 breaks in 2027. New features become incompatible with old foundations.

### Security Vulnerabilities

Critical updates don't occur. Dependencies become outdated. AlcoLab runs in browsers with known security breaches. Therefore, risk emerges silently.

### Abandoned Infrastructure

Servers fail. SSL certificates expire. Links break. Gradually, project becomes "abandonware" — ghost technology. Therefore, sustainability is critical.

## The Biggest Fear: Abandonment, Not Failure

One of the creators, Nayara, summarized the concern perfectly:

> "Our biggest fear isn't that AlcoLab fails — it's that it's abandoned from lack of support."

This isn't paranoia. It's lucidity about free software ecology. However, solutions exist. In this sense, partnerships are fundamental.

## Pursuing Partnerships: Three Strategic Fronts

To avoid the dark scenario, AlcoLab pursues **three types of partnership**:

### Scientific Credibility

International health authority partnerships confer legitimacy:

- **MSF** — global medical emergencies
- **PAHO/WHO** — Pan-American validation
- **STDF (WTO)** — international trade
- **Wellcome Trust** — global research
- **Gates Foundation** — neglected diseases
- **Mozilla Foundation** — open internet

### Sustainable Financial Support

Sustaining real operations:

- **BID Lab** — social innovation
- International health foundations

### Continuous Enhancement

Technical improvements:

- Universities collaborating on algorithms
- Global community contributing code

Therefore, multiple partnerships ensure sustainability. In sum, no scenario of abandonment.

## National Partnerships: Rooting Locally

Look at partnerships sought in Brazil:

- **Fiocruz** — national scientific drive
- **MAPA** — beverage regulation
- **Distrito Federal Health Surveillance** — public operators
- **FAP-DF, FAPEMA, FINEP** — research foundations

Furthermore, the team seeks **legislative support** from lawmakers. This creates possibility of public funding.

In this sense, this means something profound: AlcoLab doesn't want to be interesting application. It wants to be **public health infrastructure**. Therefore, strategy is to institutionalize, not commercialize. Therefore, sustainability is guaranteed.

The entire world wants this tool. Demand exists, is obvious. Therefore, supply must be maintained. Therefore, sustainability is imperative for continued impact.

## The Cost Factor: Radical Accessibility

To contextualize why open source matters:

A **gas chromatograph** costs **R$200,000+**. A **Raman spectrometer** costs **R$180,000-300,000**. Public health institutions in developing countries? Don't have budget.

**AlcoLab costs less than R$10** — basically server costs divided by users. After all, it's free.

This difference isn't marginal. It's the difference between analysis being a privilege of labs versus right of anyone. Therefore, open source is philosophy and accessibility practice.

## Why This Choice Matters Deeply

The decision to make AlcoLab open source transcends technology. It affirms that **health innovation isn't a commodity**. That human lives precede profit. That scientific transparency is non-negotiable.

It also declares: **we trust community more than property**.

But it's also pragmatic. Open source increases auditing — more eyes, fewer bugs. AGPL protects against exploitation. Zenodo immortalizes knowledge. Therefore, open source is ethics and practice simultaneously. Therefore, impact is amplified.
