## What is the difference between methanol and ethanol?

At first glance, these two alcohols seem identical. Both are colorless liquids. They have an alcoholic aroma. They present similar taste. However, this deceptive appearance hides a profound difference between methanol and ethanol.

These chemical differences determine life or death. One substance is completely safe to drink when consumed moderately. The other is a lethal poison. No amount of methanol is safe.

Understanding this difference between methanol and ethanol is not just academic curiosity. It's knowledge that can save your life or the lives of people close to you.

## Chemical structure: a crucial molecular difference

The difference begins in the most basic molecular structure. Ethanol has the chemical formula **C₂H₅OH**, while methanol has the formula **CH₃OH**.

This difference of just one carbon and two hydrogens seems minuscule. The biological consequences, however, are enormous. This molecular difference completely determines how each alcohol is metabolized in the human body.

**Compared structure:**
- **Ethanol:** CH₃-CH₂-OH (two carbons, one hydroxyl group)
- **Methanol:** CH₃-OH (one carbon, one hydroxyl group)

First and foremost, this difference affects molecular weight. It affects density. It affects viscosity. It affects how the body processes each substance. In this sense, understanding this difference between methanol and ethanol is fundamental.

## Physical properties: numbers that reveal nature

The physical properties of both differ in important ways. As demonstrated in the table below:

| Physical Property | Methanol | Ethanol | Difference |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Chemical Formula** | CH₃OH | C₂H₅OH | 1 carbon |
| **Molecular Weight (g/mol)** | 32 | 46 | 43% higher |
| **Density (g/mL at 20°C)** | 0.791 | 0.789 | Practically identical |
| **Viscosity (mPa·s at 20°C)** | 0.544 | 1.200 | Ethanol 2.2x more viscous |
| **Boiling Point (°C)** | 64.7 | 78.4 | 13.7°C difference |
| **Freezing Point (°C)** | -97 | -114 | Different |
| **Water Solubility** | Infinite | Infinite | Both miscible |

**Density:** Methanol has a density of 0.791 g/mL, while ethanol has 0.789 g/mL. Practically identical to the naked eye. Distinguishable in a laboratory with appropriate equipment. However, the difference is so small that commercial hydrometers cannot distinguish the two.

**Viscosity:** Here the difference is notable. Methanol has viscosity of 0.544 mPa·s. Ethanol has 1.200 mPa·s. Ethanol is approximately twice as viscous as methanol. This difference is key to detecting adulterations.

**Boiling Point:** Methanol boils at 64.7°C. Ethanol boils at 78.4°C. This difference of 13.7°C is significant in industrial distillation processes.

**Molecular Weight:** Methanol has a molecular weight of 32 g/mol. Ethanol has 46 g/mol. This difference affects the number of molecules in the same volume. It affects relative concentrations in mixtures.

## Impossible to distinguish by the senses

Both methanol and ethanol are colorless. Both are odorless (or have very similar odor). They are practically indistinguishable by touch. They are indistinguishable by taste. This is why many intoxication victims don't realize adulteration until symptoms appear.

A trained beverage taster can detect subtle differences in aroma and taste. An ordinary person has no chance of sensory distinction. This impossibility makes adulteration particularly dangerous. No one can visually identify methanol's presence.

First and foremost, understand: your senses don't protect you. Your vision doesn't protect you. Your sense of smell doesn't protect you. Your taste doesn't protect you. You are literally unprotected against this invisible threat.

## Metabolism: why one kills and the other doesn't

Here is the explanation of **why the difference between methanol and ethanol** determines life or death.

### Ethanol Metabolism (safe)

When you drink ethanol, your body metabolizes it through a series of controlled enzymatic reactions:

**Step 1:** Ethanol → Acetaldehyde
- Enzyme: Alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH)
- Acetaldehyde is toxic, but in small quantities

**Step 2:** Acetaldehyde → Acetic acid
- Enzyme: Aldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH)
- Acetic acid is practically harmless

**Final result:** Acetic acid is easily eliminated through breathing and urine. This is why ethanol is safe for consumption in moderate quantities. Your body can process and completely eliminate it.

### Methanol Metabolism (toxic)

Methanol follows a metabolically devastating pathway using the same enzymes:

**Step 1:** Methanol → Formaldehyde
- Enzyme: Alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH)—same enzyme
- Formaldehyde is extremely toxic
- Causes immediate tissue damage

**Step 2:** Formaldehyde → Formic acid
- Enzyme: Aldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH)—same enzyme
- Formic acid is highly toxic

**Final result:** Formic acid attacks the nervous system. It specifically damages the optic nerve. It causes severe metabolic acidosis. It harms practically all vital organs.

**This is the critical difference that explains the difference between methanol and ethanol.** Both substances use the same enzymes. But they produce completely different metabolites in terms of toxicity.

## Natural methanol: safe concentrations

Here is an important point that many don't know: methanol exists naturally in fermented beverages. Wines, beers, and distilled beverages contain small amounts of methanol. Generally less than 0.1% of alcoholic content.

**These natural quantities are completely safe.** Your body can eliminate these small doses without any problem. The liver can process them naturally. It causes no detectable damage.

Brazilian legislation (MAPA Normative Instruction 13/2005) permits up to 200 mg of methanol per 100 mL of anhydrous alcohol in distilled sugarcane beverages. These levels are completely safe when they arise naturally from the fermentation process.

Therefore, you don't need to avoid all beverages out of fear of natural methanol. The danger emerges when methanol concentration increases dramatically. Increases a hundredfold. Increases a thousandfold. Increases in criminal fashion.

## Concentrations in adulterated beverages

When criminals adulterate beverages with methanol, the concentrations are much higher than the natural ones. Studies of outbreaks show concentrations ranging from 20% to over 80% of total alcoholic composition.

In one documented 1999 outbreak, analysis of seized beverages revealed concentrations between 2.85% and 24.84% of methanol. This means each 100 mL of beverage could contain 2.85 mL to 24.84 mL of pure methanol.

**A 50 mL glass of this beverage would contain:**
- Low scenario: 1.4 mL of pure methanol
- High scenario: 12.4 mL of pure methanol
- Average: ~6-7 mL of pure methanol

Therefore, you're not just drinking adulterated ethanol. You're ingesting a concentrated poison. A potentially blinding dose. A potentially lethal dose.

## Industrial uses: why methanol exists

Both substances have completely different uses in industry:

**Industrial ethanol:**
- Alcoholic beverages—only safe use
- Fuel (alcohol fuel)—E100 or mixed with gasoline
- Disinfectants and sanitizers—personal hygiene
- Solvent in formulations—cosmetics and pharmaceuticals
- Dehydrant—chemical processes

**Industrial methanol:**
- Industrial solvent—various chemical processes
- Fuel in specific processes—not for consumption
- Formaldehyde precursor—resin synthesis
- Glass cleaning fluid—windshield antifreeze
- Chemical synthesis—pharmaceutical industry

Methanol is much cheaper than ethanol in many markets. This made adulteration an economically attractive proposition for criminals. However, in 2025, studies from the National Institute of Quality Control in Health (INCQS) and Oswaldo Cruz Foundation (Fiocruz) indicated that prices were practically equivalent in Brazil.

**This makes adulteration even more atrocious.** Criminals are deliberately choosing to use methanol despite no financial advantage. It's pure economic malice.

## How to detect adulterated beverages: warning signs

Some signs may indicate risky beverages:

**Price:** Beverages much cheaper than normal market prices may be suspicious. Although they don't always indicate adulteration. However, offers "too good to be true" should generate suspicion.

**Origin:** Beverages from unknown origin or sold at informal points have higher risk. Formalized suppliers offer traceability.

**Seal:** Beverages without original seal or with signs of previous opening should be avoided. Broken seal = possible adulteration.

**Storage:** Beverages stored in inadequate conditions (extreme heat, direct light) may be compromised. Look for cool, dark environment.

**Point of sale:** Formalized establishments with health registration offer greater safety. Street vendors don't. Informal bars have higher risk.

**Appearance:** Although invisible, anything suspicious in appearance should generate suspicion.

However, sophisticated counterfeits can fool even experts. The ideal solution is technical screening.
