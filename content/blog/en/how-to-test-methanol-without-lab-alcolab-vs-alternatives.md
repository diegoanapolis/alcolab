## How to Test Methanol Without Lab: The Real Challenge

Testing methanol in beverages is a challenge affecting health authorities, health professionals, and consumers globally. It's not curiosity — it's a critical public health problem.

All documented poisoning cases involve **20%+ methanol** in the beverage. Therefore, detection is essential. Until recently, testing methanol without a lab was practically impossible. Available techniques cost tens of thousands of dollars and exist in few specialized labs.

**Important:** How to test methanol without lab? AlcoLab is a screening tool helping confirm adulteration suspicions. However, if there are other signs of adulteration — even with normal result — we recommend not consuming the beverage.

## Gold Standard: Gas Chromatography

Gas chromatography with flame ionization detector (GC-FID) and mass spectrometry (GC-MS) are the gold standard for methanol analysis.

### Why is it the best method?

GC physically separates methanol and ethanol molecules. Identifies which alcohol is present with absolute precision. No ambiguity. No margin of error. Therefore, it's technically superior.

### The Problem: Prohibitive Cost

A gas chromatograph costs **R$30,000-80,000**. Top-end equipment exceeds **R$200,000**. Only specialized institutions have them: Fiocruz, UFSC, Unicamp.

A health surveillance agent in a small municipality never accesses this technology. Simply no municipal budget exists. Furthermore, sending samples to distant labs takes weeks. In this sense, it's precious time lost while beverage continues being sold. Therefore, chromatography is inaccessible for most.

## Portable Alternatives: Still Inaccessible

The industry developed more portable techniques for testing methanol. But they remain inaccessible for most.

### Raman Spectroscopy

Raman measures how light interacts with molecules. Portable spectrometer costs **$30,000-50,000** — or **R$180,000-300,000**.

A city government would never justify this investment. Municipalities have limited budgets. Must choose: Raman spectrometer or other health needs. Therefore, prohibitive cost remains.

### NIR Spectroscopy (Near-infrared)

Similar in price to Raman, NIR equipment costs **R$180,000-300,000**. Same cost restrictions apply. Therefore, inaccessibility is a structural problem, not a method one.

## Chemical Kits: Unfulfilled Promises

Some methanol detection kits exist. But face significant problems.

### Kit Limitations

Many are still in development. Depend on patents restricting availability. Use toxic chemicals requiring careful handling. Availability is extremely limited — you won't find them in common pharmacies.

A health surveillance agent can't depend on a kit they can't acquire in emergency. Therefore, kits don't solve structural access problem. After all, screening must be available when needed, not when manufacturer decides to sell.
