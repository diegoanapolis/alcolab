# Methanol poisoning worldwide: global crisis

Methanol intoxication is a public health emergency affecting practically all continents. Since 1998, approximately 40,100 people have been intoxicated globally, resulting in 14,323 confirmed deaths. Médecins Sans Frontières (MSF) documented methanol intoxication in 185 countries.

Mortality rate in outbreaks varies 20-40%, depending on medical access. In regions with limited infrastructure, rates can exceed 50%. WHO estimates 21% of alcohol consumed globally comes from illegal sources. An environment conducive to adulteration and methanol poisoning worldwide.

## Why methanol continues to be a global problem

**Socioeconomic factors:**
- Extreme poverty forces consumption of cheap beverages
- High taxes incentivize black markets
- Illegal establishments operate with impunity

**Critical regulatory gap:** 155 countries (73% of nations) lack laboratory infrastructure. Cases never documented. Governments underestimate the problem. **Therefore**, methanol poisoning worldwide is underreported.

**Counterfeiting technology evolves:**
- Increasingly realistic labels
- Bottles, caps, seals reproduced with precision
- Home distillation contains dangerous methanol
- Methanol frequently a byproduct, not intentional addition

**Challenge:** counterfeiters surpass regulation with advanced technology.

## Community protection

**Individual strategies:**
- Buy only from licensed establishments
- Always request receipts
- Look for integrity seals on bottles
- Distrust abnormally low prices
- Inspect label printing closely
- Compare with known legitimate bottles
- Watch for unusual colors, smells, tastes
- Never share unknown beverages

**Signs of intoxication (12-48 hours):**
- Intense headache
- Blurred vision or vision loss (critical)
- Nausea and vomiting
- Abdominal pain
- Difficulty breathing

**Critical action:** seek emergency immediately. Vision loss distinctive of methanol. Rapid action significantly increases survival chances.
